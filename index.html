Introduction

A Few Companies, Billions of Users

When you search for something, scroll your feed, or open a news app, you are almost always using a product built by an American tech company. This guide explains why that matters — for you, for journalism, and for democracy.

Google, Apple, Meta (which owns Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp), Amazon, and Microsoft are sometimes called the "Big Tech" companies. They are all based in the United States — part of the English-speaking world. Together, they design the search engines, social media platforms, and operating systems that the vast majority of the world's internet users depend on every day.

Because these platforms are controlled by just a few companies, they have enormous power over two things that matter in a democracy: privacy (what data about you is collected and how it is used) and news (what information you see and which stories reach you). Their algorithms — the computer systems that decide what to show you — can also make societies more divided.

Thesis

Because these platforms are controlled by a small number of English-speaking companies, they shape global news and privacy, and their algorithms can make societies more politically divided.

Information Control

How Tech Giants Control Information Flow

The "flow of information" — what you see, read, and believe — is shaped by a small number of companies, not by independent national media systems alone.

Google and Search: When you type a question into Google, its algorithm decides which websites appear first. For most countries, Google controls more than 90% of all web searches. This means Google effectively decides what information billions of people encounter on any given topic — including news, politics, and health.

Meta and News Sharing: Facebook and Instagram are where many people share and discover news. Editors at news organisations know that if Meta's algorithm stops promoting their stories, they can lose the majority of their online readership overnight. This gives Meta enormous, indirect power over what journalism looks like.

Apple and Google App Stores: Both companies act as "gatekeepers" for smartphones. A news app that is removed from both stores essentially cannot reach mobile users. This gives Apple and Google quiet but real power over which information services can exist and thrive.

Key Argument

The "flow of information" is no longer controlled only by governments or traditional media — it is shaped by a handful of private, American companies whose primary goal is profit, not the public interest.

📝
Worth Remembering
News is no longer only chosen by editors; it is also shaped by which companies control distribution and advertising money.
Privacy

Effects on Global Privacy

Tech companies collect vast amounts of personal data. This raises serious questions about surveillance, manipulation, and who really owns information about your life.

What data is collected?

  • 🔍
    Search historyEvery query you type into Google — what you're curious about, worried about, or shopping for.
  • 👍
    Likes, posts, and commentsEverything you interact with on Facebook and Instagram reveals your opinions and interests.
  • 📍
    Location dataYour phone tracks where you are — and tech platforms often collect and store this continuously.
  • 📱
    App usage patternsWhich apps you use, when, and for how long — a detailed map of your daily life.
  • 🛒
    Purchase and browsing historyWhat you look at online, even if you don't buy — used to build detailed consumer profiles.

This data is primarily used to target advertisements — to show you ads for products you are likely to buy. But it also raises deeper concerns: surveillance by governments, data breaches that expose private information, and the risk that companies use data in ways users never agreed to.

Amnesty International has described these data practices as a threat to human rights, arguing that constant profiling and personalisation can affect not just what you buy, but what you believe and how you see the world.

Meta in particular has been criticised for tracking users even when they have said no to tracking — for example, by using tools that follow you across websites and apps you do not even own.

🇪🇺
The GDPR Solution

In 2018, the European Union introduced the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — one of the world's strongest privacy laws. It gives EU citizens the right to know what data is collected, the right to delete it, and the right to say no. It also forces companies to ask for clear consent before tracking users.

However, the same companies often apply weaker standards in countries without similar laws — especially in the Global South. This creates a global privacy gap: your rights depend on where you live, even though the same company is collecting your data.

⭐
Key Insight
The same company can treat European users more strictly than users in regions with weaker laws — this "global privacy gap" reveals a deeply unequal distribution of digital rights.
Journalism

Effects on Global News

Tech platforms have not just changed how we access news — they have changed the economics of journalism itself, making media organisations dependent on Big Tech.

Who decides what you read? When you open Facebook or Google News, an algorithm — not a human editor — decides which articles appear at the top of your screen. This means that the news millions of people see every day is filtered by a company whose primary interest is keeping you engaged, not informing you.

Economic dependence: Most news organisations today depend on Google and Meta for two things: traffic (readers clicking through to their sites) and advertising revenue. When these platforms change their algorithms or reduce payments to news publishers, entire newsrooms can be devastated. The Brookings Institution has described this as a deeply unequal "frenemies" relationship, where journalism depends on tech but tech has all the power.

Media environment capture: Some critics use the term "media environment capture" to describe the situation where tech platforms shape the entire news ecosystem — not just distributing news, but deciding what kind of journalism gets rewarded, which topics trend, and which stories disappear.

Key Term

"Media environment capture" — when tech platforms shape the whole news ecosystem instead of just being neutral tools for distribution.

Pew Research Finding

Surveys across multiple countries show that many people believe social media makes it easier for misinformation and rumours to spread. When algorithms promote emotional or sensational content (because it gets more clicks), false stories can reach millions of people before fact-checkers respond.

Algorithms

Algorithms and Polarisation

The computer systems that decide what you see are not neutral. They are designed to keep you scrolling — and that can divide societies.

01
The Algorithm Watches You

Every like, share, comment, and second you spend on a post is recorded. The algorithm builds a model of what makes you react.

02
It Shows You More of the Same

To maximise your engagement, the algorithm shows you content similar to what you already liked — opinions that match yours, stories that confirm your views.

03
Echo Chambers Form

Over time, you mainly see one type of opinion. You encounter fewer people who think differently. Your "filter bubble" keeps opposing views out of sight.

04
Extreme Content Gets Boosted

Emotional and outrage-driven posts get the most reactions. The algorithm interprets high engagement as quality — so extreme content rises to the top.

05
Political Polarisation Increases

Research shows that people who get their news primarily through social media are more likely to see political opponents as enemies rather than fellow citizens.

Core Argument

"Algorithms prioritise attention, not truth — and that can divide societies instead of helping them understand each other."

The key distinction is between what algorithms are designed to do (maximise engagement) and what the side effect can be (polarisation). The companies did not set out to divide society — but their business model of attention-driven advertising creates exactly that incentive.

This is sometimes described as "engagement-driven design": platforms are built to be as addictive as possible, and emotional, divisive content is more addictive than calm, nuanced reporting.

💡
Note
Echo chamber and filter bubble are related but different. An echo chamber is social — you surround yourself with like-minded people. A filter bubble is algorithmic — the platform filters out other views automatically.
Glossary

Key Concepts

These are the core terms used when discussing Big Tech and its impact on society. Understanding them helps you follow the debate — wherever you encounter it.

Big Tech / Tech Giants The largest technology companies — primarily Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon, and Microsoft — that dominate global digital infrastructure.
Information Flow How news and facts move from sources to audiences. Big Tech companies now control a large part of this flow.
Personal Data / Surveillance Information collected about you — your searches, location, and behaviour — used by companies to target advertising.
GDPR General Data Protection Regulation. EU law that gives citizens control over their personal data and limits how companies can use it.
Global Privacy Gap The fact that the same company applies stronger privacy rules in the EU than in countries with weaker laws, creating unequal protection worldwide.
Media Environment Capture When tech platforms shape the whole news ecosystem — not just distributing news, but deciding which journalism gets rewarded.
Echo Chamber A social environment where a person only encounters opinions that agree with their own, reinforcing existing beliefs.
Filter Bubble When an algorithm customises your feed to match your preferences, filtering out content that challenges your worldview.
Algorithmic Polarisation The process by which engagement-driven algorithms increase political division by amplifying extreme and emotional content.
Engagement-Driven Design Platforms built to maximise the time you spend on them — prioritising emotional content because it generates more reactions.
Gatekeeping Controlling who can access what. Apple and Google "gatekeep" the App Store, deciding which news services can reach smartphone users.
Misinformation False information. Algorithms can spread misinformation rapidly because emotional false stories often get more engagement than accurate reporting.
Conclusion

Putting It All Together

A summary of the main arguments — and why this topic matters beyond any classroom.

In conclusion, the fact that most major tech companies come from the English-speaking world means that they have enormous power over global privacy and news. They collect vast amounts of personal data, help deliver news to billions of people, and use algorithms that keep users scrolling.

This can be beneficial: these platforms connect people across borders and give access to information that was previously hard to find. However, the downsides are serious. Privacy protections are unequal across the world. News organisations have become economically dependent on tech platforms. And algorithms increase polarisation by showing people only what they already agree with.

Therefore, the model of a few global companies shaping the world's information flow raises important questions about who really controls our privacy, our news, and our public debate today.

✅
The Big Picture
A handful of private companies now shape what billions of people read, believe, and share every day. Understanding how — and why it matters — is one of the most important things you can do as a citizen of the digital world.
